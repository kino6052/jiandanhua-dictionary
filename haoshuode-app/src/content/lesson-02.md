```vocab
dōngxi | thing, something, being | | 东西
rén | person, human | | 人
shuǐguǒ | fruit, vegetable | | 水果
xiě | write | | 写
nǚrén | woman, female | | 女人
zhè | this | | 这
dòngwù | animal, land mammal | | 动物
```

Look again at <audio-example zh="东西">dōngxi</audio-example> and <audio-example zh="女人">nǚrén</audio-example> above. <audio-example zh="东西">dōngxi</audio-example> is built from <audio-example zh="东">dōng</audio-example> (east) and <audio-example zh="西">xi</audio-example> (west) — yet it means "thing," with no geography left in it. <audio-example zh="女人">nǚrén</audio-example> is more transparent: <audio-example zh="女">nǚ</audio-example> (female) plus <audio-example zh="人">rén</audio-example> (person) gives you "woman" plainly. Chinese words are built from roots the same way English words are — `understand` is `under` plus `stand`, `telephone` is `far` plus `sound` — but a fluent speaker doesn't reassemble the parts to get the meaning. A word is learned whole; its roots are history, not a formula you run each time you use it.

Hao-shuo-de treats its dictionary the same way: every word on the list is memorized whole, roots and all. What Hao-shuo-de won't do is let you mint new words that way. If you need a meaning the dictionary doesn't have, you won't fuse two roots into something that reads like a new solid word — you'll say it explicitly, with a hyphen.

- **No Plurals:** By themselves, Hao-shuo-de nouns are completely number-neutral. Context dictates the volume. For example, the word <audio-example zh="女人">nǚrén</audio-example> can mean either "woman" or "women" depending on the situation. If you ever need to make "many" explicit, that job belongs to a separate word placed elsewhere in the sentence — never to the noun itself. The noun's shape never changes no matter how many you mean; only the sentence around it can say so. More on this in the next chapter.
- **Pointing to Things:** To say "this" or "that," Hao-shuo-de links the demonstrative pointers (<audio-example zh="这">zhè</audio-example> or <audio-example zh="那">nà</audio-example>) directly to the universal measure word <audio-example zh="个">gè</audio-example> using a hyphen (<audio-example zh="这个">zhè-ge</audio-example> / <audio-example zh="那个">nà-ge</audio-example>).
- **Why the Extra Word?** <audio-example zh="人">rén</audio-example> by itself names the idea of "person" — not one particular person you could point at. To point at one, Chinese needs a small word that turns the idea into something countable, the same way English says "a **sheet** of paper" instead of just "a paper." Chinese does this for every noun, not only special cases, so the piece can't be skipped — <audio-example zh="这人">zhè rén</audio-example> reaches for something that isn't there yet; <audio-example zh="这个人">zhè-ge rén</audio-example> finishes the thought. Real Mandarin actually has dozens of these counting words, each tied to a noun's shape — flat things, long things, animals, bound books, and so on — and picking the right one is one of the genuinely hard parts of learning Chinese. Hao-shuo-de skips that: <audio-example zh="个">gè</audio-example> counts everything, every time. You'll never have to guess which one a noun takes, because there's only ever one.
- **Action-to-Noun Building:** In Mandarin, you can easily build nouns out of verbs by adding the particle <audio-example zh="的">de</audio-example>. Because Hao-shuo-de avoids cluttering your brain with separate specialized words for "book," "paper," or "document," these concepts are elegantly expressed as a compound unit meaning **"a written thing"** (<audio-example zh="写的东西">xiě-de dōngxi</audio-example>).
- **Particles That Bind, Particles That Don't:** Every hyphen in this book marks the same thing: a grammar piece fused onto a word — <audio-example zh="个">-ge</audio-example>, <audio-example zh="的">-de</audio-example>, and others still to come. Say the block out loud and it moves as one unit, the way <audio-example zh="这个">zhè-ge</audio-example> or <audio-example zh="写的">xiě-de</audio-example> does. Other grammar words — like the one marking a finished action, or the one marking a question — stand on their own instead, with a space on both sides, because they belong to the whole sentence, not to any single word beside them. You'll meet those one at a time, lesson by lesson. For now: **if a hyphen glues it to a word, it's shaping that word; if it stands free, it's shaping the sentence.**
