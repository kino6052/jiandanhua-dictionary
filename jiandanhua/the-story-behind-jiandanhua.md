_Why Mandarin needed a "minimum viable version" of itself, and why that version has to be real Mandarin — not an invented substitute._

## Stuck on the Mountain

If you have been learning Mandarin for a long time and still feel stuck, you are not imagining it. Mandarin is routinely described as one of the hardest languages for an English speaker to learn, and the difficulty is not evenly distributed. It comes in a small number of extremely hard bottlenecks, wrapped in a much larger amount of ordinary, learnable material. Conventional courses do not separate the two. They hand you the whole mountain at once — characters, tones, an unfamiliar grammar, and a vocabulary whose logic does not map cleanly onto English — and expect you to climb all of it simultaneously. Most people do not fail because they lack discipline. They fail because no one gave them a route.

This article is about where the idea for a different route — 简单话 (Jiǎndānhuà) — actually came from. It did not start as a linguistics project. It started at a Toastmasters club.

## What Toastmasters Got Right

I am a member of Toastmasters, and I am close to finishing my first Pathway level. What struck me, watching the club work, was how effective the structure is at producing real engagement: prepared speeches, unprepared Table Topics questions, structured feedback from an evaluator, rotating roles that force everyone to both perform and support. Nobody is a passive spectator. You think on your feet, you prepare, you play a role, and you improve — visibly, on a schedule, in front of people who are doing the same thing.

The obvious next thought was: why doesn't language learning work like this? Why is there no club where the same structure — prepared talks, spontaneous questions, structured feedback, rotating roles — is applied directly to speaking Mandarin?

I wrote up that idea first, as a piece called _The Language Circle_. But while designing it, I ran into a wall that any experienced language learner would recognize immediately.

## Why You Can't Just "Speak Whatever Mandarin You Have"

The obvious version of a Mandarin Toastmasters club is: everyone shows up and speaks Mandarin at whatever level they've got. In practice, this fails for two specific, well-known reasons.

The first is listening comprehension. Mandarin at native speed, with tone sandhi and a huge space of homophones, is very hard to parse for anyone below an advanced level — far harder, relative to vocabulary size, than the equivalent gap in most European languages.

The second is vocabulary that doesn't map cleanly onto the logic of English or other Western languages. A word like `一下` doesn't have a stable English handle. Kinship terms encode generational and paternal/maternal distinctions with no English equivalent. Classifiers, aspect particles, and topic-comment structure require re-building intuitions from scratch rather than translating them.

The combined effect in a mixed-level room is clustering. People who are already fluent enough to survive at native speed drift together. People who aren't, disengage or fall silent. The club structure that makes Toastmasters work — mixed levels, rotating roles, everyone producing language under real conditions — quietly collapses, because there is no shared level of the language that the whole room can actually use.

That gap is where the idea of a bridge language came from. Not from a linguistic research agenda, and not from the kind of philosophical motivation that led Toki Pona's creator to design a language around minimalism as a way of life. The need was purely mechanical: a club needs a layer of the language that everyone in the room can speak completely, so that people at different levels can still practice together instead of clustering apart.

## The Software Insight

Here is the part of the idea that actually made it click for me. Mandarin's difficulty is not a single, uniform wall — it is several independent concerns bundled together: a writing system (characters), a phonological system (tones, homophones), a grammar, and a vocabulary. Conventional instruction attacks all four at once, which is exactly the kind of design mistake that software engineering has a name for: a failure to separate concerns.

In software, when a system becomes too tangled to build or reason about, you don't attack the whole thing simultaneously. You find the minimal, necessary-and-sufficient core that everything else depends on — the smallest set of primitives from which everything higher up can be composed — and you get that core right first. Everything else is then built as a controlled extension of that core, not a wholesale second attack on the whole problem.

Applied to Mandarin, that means: identify the minimal atomic vocabulary — the smallest set of words that is both necessary and sufficient to express real, native-level ideas by qualifying and composing them — strip out the writing system temporarily by using pinyin, and keep the grammar completely intact and completely real. Learn that core to fluency first. Everything else — characters, the full vocabulary, native-speed listening — becomes a controlled extension of a foundation you already own, instead of one more front in a war you're already losing on three others.

## Why It Has to Be Real Mandarin

There is a version of this idea that is easier to build and much more tempting: design a new, simplified conlang inspired by Toki Pona, using Mandarin words but a hand-built, simplified grammar of your own invention. I considered this seriously, and rejected it, for one reason: if the grammar and the word choices aren't genuinely valid Mandarin, you are not building a bridge — you are building an island. You'll be fluent in a language that only your small circle understands, and every hour invested in it will need to be re-learned, not extended, the moment you try to speak with an actual native speaker.

So the rule for Jiǎndānhuà is strict: every sentence must be completely valid, grammatically correct Mandarin — just built from a distilled, high-leverage subset of the vocabulary. Nothing you learn has to be unlearned later. It only has to be added to.

## An Honest Experiment

I want to be clear about what this is: an experiment, not a proven method. I don't have data showing this approach works better than the alternatives — I have an argument, drawn from watching a club structure succeed at a related problem, and from the well-worn software principle that hard systems get built by isolating a minimal core first. I think the argument is strong enough to be worth testing. At worst, building it will produce real insight into what actually blocks Mandarin learners. At best, it becomes a genuinely new paradigm for how a hard language gets learned.

The plan has three layers. 好话 (Hǎohuà) is a Toki-Pona-scale core — a tiny, strictly valid subset of Mandarin words and grammar, small enough to reach fluency in quickly. 简单话 (Jiǎndānhuà) is a strict superset of it: a fuller, still-distilled vocabulary — on the order of a thousand words — capable of expressing real adult ideas without artificial restriction. Full Mandarin is the superset of both. You never backtrack. You only extend.

If you've been stuck on Mandarin for a long time, this might be the route up the mountain you've been missing. You can find the specification, the vocabulary, and getting-started material at [jiandanhua.org](https://jiandanhua.org).
